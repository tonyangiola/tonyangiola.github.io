“What is a Snippy?” A surprising question from a well-known figure in the UFO/UAP world rekindled the San Luis Valley’s most pivotal case — Snippy the Horse.

The Valley of Shadows

The San Luis Valley has long been a hotspot for high strangeness, but it wasn’t until the Snippy case—the first animal mutilation to gain worldwide attention—that its reputation became widely recognized. Agnes King lived on the King Ranch with her son Harry… residents reported strange lights near Mount Blanca and beyond.

The Horses

In 1967, Snippy was a 3-year-old Appaloosa gelding… given to Nellie Lewis for her birthday and later brought to the Valley, splitting time between the Lewis cabin and the nearby King Ranch.

The King Ranch pastured four horses: Smokey (Black), Sheiba (Sorrel), Lady (Dapple Grey), and Snippy (Appaloosa)… Next door, the Wellington Ranch (“Dead Cow Ranch”) had repeated mutilations long before the word was common.

Confusion over Identity

APRO files and later retellings muddled Snippy’s name and sex, sometimes conflating him with Lady. Contemporary photos and the bones themselves resolved this: Snippy was male, confirmed by hip structure and by eyewitness Leona Wellington.

The Death of Smokey

About a week before Snippy’s mutilation, Smokey was found dead near the same pasture. Lacking investigation, it was chalked up to a rattlesnake bite — a poor fit for a horse his size without a neck/nose strike.

Discovery of Snippy

On Sept 9, 1967, after rain left fresh tracks, Harry King found Snippy about a quarter mile from the ranch house. All flesh from the neck up was gone, the collar showed a clean cauterized incision, and an overpowering medicinal odor hung in the air. Bones shifted in color over days: pink, then tar black.

Forensic Findings

Dr. Altshuler of Alamosa examined the remains a week later: vertebrae and skull cavity were completely dry; all organs removed without blood; a small white powder deposit in the stomach; right hind leg likely broken as if dropped from above; left hind stripped to bone.

Environmental Anomalies

Investigators recorded green ooze near the body, an odd “tool” oozing the same substance, scorch marks, clusters of cylindrical holes, paired heavy imprints, and three fence posts severed as by extreme heat. APRO noted a 75-ft circle with five 15-ft circles around it; sand inside was smooth with rain divots outside — implying the objects were present during a shower.

Aftermath

Earnest Wellington died suddenly weeks later; an APRO investigator died within a year. Reports continued: bloodless carcasses, craft sightings, humming, missing time, glowing orbs, black boxes, and even a shop visitor who seemingly lacked “earth money.” The Snippy case marked a pattern that would haunt the Valley.
